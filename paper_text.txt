We study the Knapsack Problem with Conﬂicts, a generalization of the Knapsack Problem in which a set
of conﬂicts speciﬁes pairs of items which cannot be simultaneously selected. In this work, we propose a
novel combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithm for this problem based on an n-ary branching scheme.
Our algorithm effectively combines different procedures for pruning the branch-and-bound nodes based
on different relaxations of the Knapsack Problem with Conﬂicts. Its main elements of novelty are: (i)
the adoption of the branching-and-pruned set branching scheme which, while extensively used in the
maximum-clique literature, was never successfully employed for solving the Knapsack Problem with Conﬂicts; (ii) the adoption of the Multiple-Choice Knapsack Problem for the derivation of upper bounds used
for pruning the branch-and-bound tree nodes; and (iii) the design of a new upper bound for the latter
problem which can be computed very eﬃciently. Key to our algorithm is its high pruning potential and
the low computational effort that it requires to process each branch-and-bound node. An extensive set
of experiments carried out on the benchmark instances typically used in the literature shows that, for
edge densities ranging from 0.1 to 0.9, our algorithm is faster by up to two orders of magnitude than the
state-of-the-art method and by up to several orders of magnitude than a state-of-the-art mixed-integer
linear programming solver.

1. Introduction
Given a set V of n items with a positive integer proﬁt pi and
a positive integer weight wi , for all i ∈ V, and an integer capacity
c, the classical Knapsack Problem (KP) asks for identifying a subset of items of maximum proﬁt whose total weight is no larger
than c. The KP is one of the most studied problems in combinatorial optimization. The problem is known to be N P -hard (albeit
weakly) since the seminal work of Karp (1972), and very effective
algorithms are available for its solution (Martello, Pisinger, & Toth,
1999). For a comprehensive survey on algorithms and applications,
we refer the reader to Kellerer, Pferschy, and Pisinger (2004) and
Martello and Toth (1990).
In this paper, we study a generalization of the KP known as
the Knapsack Problem with Conﬂicts (KPC) (or the Knapsack Problem with Conﬂict Graph) (Bettinelli, Cacchiani, & Malaguti, 2017).

The KPC plays an important role in the Operations Research
literature. It arises as the pricing subproblem when solving with

(with a Ryan-Foster branching scheme) (Wei, Luo, Baldacci, & Lim,
2019) and the Bin Packing Problem with Conﬂicts (BPPC) (with
any branching scheme) (Bettinelli et al., 2017). Besides its role as
a subproblem, the KPC is of interest on its own right. Real-world
applications arise in any context where a subset of items has to
be selected subject to a capacity (or budget) constraint and a set

1.3. Contributions and outline of the paper
In this work, we propose a novel branch-and-bound algorithm
for solving large instances of the KPC which employs an n-ary
branching scheme based on the “branching and pruned set” approach. We refer to this branch-and-bound method as “combinatorial” since, as we will illustrate, it does not rely on mathematical
programming techniques for pruning the nodes of the tree. This is
in line with the usage of the term done in other papers, including (Louveaux & Mathieu, 2014). Our method effectively combines
the two main aspects of the KPC, its capacity (or KP) aspect and its
conﬂict (or MWISP) aspect, by relying on different bounding procedures for pruning the individual branch-and-bound nodes as well
as for reducing the number of child nodes that are generated during the branching phase. Moreover, the algorithm is the ﬁrst, to
the best of our knowledge, to adopt the Multiple-Choice Knapsack
Problem for the derivation of upper bounds. In particular, we design a new MCKP-based upper bound, which we refer to as partition bound, that can be computed very eﬃciently in practice.
The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 summarizes
previous works on the KPC and on closely related problems.
Section 3 introduces three Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulations for the KPC. The new branch-and-bound algorithm that

2. Previous and related works
The KPC is originally introduced in Yamada, Kataoka, and
Watanabe (2002), where the authors propose a local-search and a
branch-and-bound method, the latter based on the Lagrangian relaxation obtained by dualizing the conﬂict constraints. In Hiﬁ and
Michrafy (2007), a method is proposed which combines a primal
heuristic with a ﬁxing procedure based on the lower bound provided by the former to reduce the problem size, after which the
(reduced) problem is solved via an ILP-based branch-and-bound algorithm. A reactive-search method is proposed in Hiﬁ and Michrafy
(2006), whereas Akeb, Hiﬁ, and Mounir (2011) introduces a heuristic based on an integer programming formulation which also relies
on the notion of local branching (Fischetti & Lodi, 2003). In Hiﬁ
and Otmani (2012) and Hiﬁ (2014), a scatter-search and an iterative rounding search-based heuristic are proposed, respectively.
The state-of-the-art exact algorithm for solving large instances of
the KPC is presented in Bettinelli et al. (2017). It consists of a
branch-and-bound algorithm based on binary branching, and it relies on a fathoming procedure which combines a bounding technique often used for solving the MWISP, see, e.g, Held, Cook, and
Sewell (2012), with the KP aspect of the KPC.
Some work has been carried out for the case where the graph
G has a special topology. In particular, Pferschy and Schauer
(2009) presents pseudopolynomial-time algorithms capable of producing exact solutions when G is either a chordal graph or a graph
with bounded tree width, and illustrates how to extend such algorithms to obtain fully polynomial-time approximation schemes. In
contrast to the MWISP, which is polynomial-time solvable on perfect graphs (Grötschel, Lovász, & Schrijver, 1981), the same paper
shows that the KPC is strongly N P -hard when G is perfect. This
property does not hold for every perfect graph though. Indeed, as
shown in Sadykov and Vanderbeck (2013), the KPC is weakly N P hard when G is an interval graph.
As we mentioned, the KPC arises as pricing subproblem when
solving with branch-and-price algorithms the BPP and the BPPC
(adopting, for the former, a Ryan-Forster branching scheme). In
this context, Muritiba, Iori, Malaguti, and Toth (2010) proposes
a two-step algorithm which ﬁrst applies a greedy heuristic and
then, if no column with negative reduced cost has been found,
resorts to solving the KPC with a state-of-the-art ILP solver. In
Elhedhli, Li, Gzara, and Naoum-Sawaya (2011), the KPC is tackled by solving it directly using an ILP formulation. In Sadykov
and Vanderbeck (2013), an exact approach based on a depth-ﬁrst
branch-and-bound algorithm is proposed and, for the case where G
is an interval graph, a pseudopolynomial-time algorithm based on
dynamic programming. In Wei et al. (2019), an exact (exponentialtime in the worst case) label-setting algorithm is proposed which
relies on fathoming and dominance rules to reduce the search
space. To the best of our knowledge, the latter constitutes the state
of the art when tackling the special type of KPC instances which
arise when solving the BPP/BPPC with a branch-and-price method.
As the authors of Wei et al. (2019) state, their algorithm does not
compete with the one proposed in Bettinelli et al. (2017) when
tested on the (harder) instances used in the latter work.

et al. (2019) are the state-of-the-art exact algorithms for the cases
with weights on the vertices and on the edges, respectively.

4. A new combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithm
In this section, we describe our new combinatorial branch-andbound algorithm for solving the KPC. The algorithm is based on an
n-ary branching scheme relying on the notion of “branching and
pruned sets”, which we deﬁne in what follows. In particular, we
rely on several effective bounding techniques to reduce the size of
the branching tree by pruning its nodes. This includes the adoption
of a bound derived from the Multiple-Choice Knapsack Problem,
which, to the best of our knowledge, was never successfully used
for solving the KPC.
The nodes of the branch-and-bound tree are obtained by adding
to the current solution one item at a time in a recursive fashion,
avoiding any conﬂicting items and guaranteeing that the capacity
constraint be satisﬁed. Given a node of the tree, let Iˆ ⊆ V be the
independent set corresponding to the partial solution associated
with it. Let also LB be the value of the incumbent solution Iinc . If,
at any node, p(Iˆ) > LB holds, both Iinc and LB are updated.
We denote by Vˆ the set of vertices not in Iˆ with the property
that, if any vertex i ∈ Vˆ is individually added to Iˆ, the resulting set
Iˆ ∪ {i} remains an independent set. Formally, Vˆ corresponds to the
intersection of the antineighborhoods of the vertices in Iˆ, i.e.:

Our branching scheme relies on partitioning the vertex set Vˆ
into two subsets: the branching set B and the pruned set P. Similar
branching schemes are used in the majority of the most effective
combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithms for the MWISP/MWCP
and their variants, see, e.g., Jiang, Li, and Manyà (2017), Li, Fang,
Jiang, and Xu (2018); Li, Jiang, and Manyà (2017); Li, Liu, Jiang,
Many, and Li (2018), San Segundo and Tapia (2014), and San Segundo, Furini et al. (2019); San Segundo, Matia, Rodriguez-Losada,
and Hernando (2013); San Segundo, Nikolaev, and Batsyn (2015);

San Segundo, Nikolaev, Batsyn, and Pardalos (2016); San Segundo
et al. (2011). To the best of our knowledge, such a branching
scheme was never successfully adopted in the context of branchand-bound algorithms for solving the KPC. The overarching idea is
to determine a set P ⊆ Vˆ of vertices such that the value LB of the
incumbent solution Iinc cannot be improved by adding any feasible subset of P to it. For a given set P, the set B is deﬁned as
P’s complement, i.e., B := Vˆ \ P . Since, by deﬁnition of P, at least
one item from B is necessary to improve the incumbent solution
Iinc , branching on any of the vertices in P at the current node of
the tree can be avoided. This way, the branching operations that
are carried out are more likely to lead to an improvement of LB,
which, in turn, is likely to lead to a smaller branch-and-bound tree
due to a more effective pruning. As a consequence, the larger P the
smaller the number of child nodes of a given branch-and-bound
node.
After P and, consequently, B, have been determined, we carry
out a |B|-ary branching operation, creating a tree node per vertex
i ∈ B by adding i to Iˆ. The effective procedure we adopt to construct
a “small” branching set B and, therefore, a “large” pruned set P is
described in the next subsection.

would be obtained by adding i to C, the value of budget would
be nonnegative. If both conditions hold, vertex i is removed from
B and added to C and P. If not, vertex i remains in B and the next
vertex is examined. The procedure halts when either (i) no additional vertices in B can be added to P without resulting in a negative budget (which implies that no more vertices can be added
to C without violating the invariant) or (ii) P = Vˆ . Whenever the
procedure ﬁnds that adding a vertex i ∈ B to C would result in a
negative budget, i is discarded. This is because, by design of the
algorithm, such vertex would lead to a negative budget even if it
were added to one of the cliques the procedure is yet to construct.
The PARTITION procedure considers the vertices in Vˆ in reverse proﬁt-over-weight order (i.e., in reverse order w.r.t. the order in (1)). This way, P is more likely to contain vertices with low
proﬁt-over-weight ratio, leaving those with a higher ratio in the

439

set B. As we branch by adding the vertices in B to the current solution Iˆ one at a time, with this choice we are more likely to obtain
solutions with high proﬁt at the earlier stages of the algorithm.
5. Bounding procedures for pruning individual nodes
In our algorithm, we consider different bounds for pruning individual nodes of the branch-and-bound tree. They are of two families. The ﬁrst family is based on the KP relaxation of the KPC that
is obtained by dropping the conﬂict constraints. The second family relies on the Multiple-Choice Knapsack Problem (MCKP), a special case of the KPC in which the conﬂict graph is a collection of
pairwise disjoint cliques. Notice that, while, traditionally, the MCKP
asks for selecting exactly one item per clique, the problem asking
for selecting at most an item per clique can be reduced to the traditional one by adding a dummy item to each clique with 0 proﬁt
and 0 weight. The MCKP has been widely studied in the literature,
see, e.g., Pisinger (1995) and Sinha and Zoltners (1979), and Ch. 11
of Kellerer et al. (2004). To the best of our knowledge, it was never
used for deriving bounds in the context of algorithms for solving
the KPC.
We note that, in spite of the KPC being strongly N P -hard, all
the bounds we consider in our algorithm correspond to weakly
N P -hard problems (the KP and the MCKP) and to relaxations
of either of them which are computable in polynomial time.
As better explained in the following, in our algorithm we compute the polynomial-time bounds at each branch-and-bound node,
while we compute the weakly N P -hard ones only once (at the
root node) in such a way that they are valid in every node of
the branch-and-bound tree, and store them in lookup-tables (see
Sections 5.1.1 and 5.2.1).
All the upper bounds that we introduce in the following are
valid for the instance of the KPC corresponding to the current
branch-and-bound node with vertex set Vˆ and residual capacity
c (Vˆ ), i.e., for KPC (Vˆ , c (Vˆ )). A branch-and-bound node with vertex
set Vˆ can be pruned whenever the upper bound on KPC (Vˆ , c (Vˆ ))
is less or equal than LB − p(Iˆ). This is because, if KPC (Vˆ , c (Vˆ )) ≤
LB − p(Iˆ), no subset of vertices in Vˆ suﬃces to create a feasible
solution better than the incumbent.

6. Outline of the algorithm and demonstration
In this section, we provide an outline of our new combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithm, which we refer to as CFS (the
acronym corresponds to the initials of the last names of the three
authors of this work). This algorithm consists of two phases: (i) a
preprocessing phase in which we compute an initial feasible solution and we reduce the size of the input graph by pegging some of
the vertices (see Section 6.1); (ii) a branch-and-bound procedure
described in the previous sections which ﬁnds an optimal solution
to the problem (see Section 6.2 for the implementation details).
6.1. Preprocessing phase and heuristic algorithm
Before running the branch-and-bound procedure, CFS computes a heuristic solution by resorting to two simple and fast
heuristics. The ﬁrst one is a greedy heuristic which considers
the items in nonincreasing order of proﬁt-over-weight ratio and
adds them to the solution only if they do not conﬂict with the
previously-added ones and as long as the capacity is not exceeded.
We run this heuristic n times, forcing, each time, one of the items
in V to be in the solution and storing the best solution found. The
second heuristic is a diving heuristic guided by the LP relaxation
of ILP2 (which can be computed very eﬃciently, see Table 4). Starting from the empty solution, the method iteratively solves the LP
relaxation and checks whether the ﬁrst (in order of proﬁt-overweight ratio) fractional item can be added to the current solution
without violating any constraints. If this is the case, the item is
added to the current solution, whereas, if not, it is forced to be out
of it. The method stops when no more fractional items are available, thus ﬁnding a feasible heuristic solution.

After the two heuristics have been run, the initial lower bound
LB is then set to the value of the best solution found by either of
them. Then, we compute the MCKP-based lookup-table described
in Section 5.2.1. The corresponding bounds are then used in every
branch-and-bound node of the tree.
The algorithm performs then an additional phase with the goal
of pegging some items, i.e., either eliminating them from the instance or forcing them to be in the solution of the problem. For
each item i ∈ V, we consider the following ﬁrst pegging condition:

LB ≥ UB(N (i ), c − wi ) + pi ,

(22)

where as upper bound we adopt the one which, among the ones
we introduced, is the tightest on the problem instance restricted to
the items in N(i) and to a capacity of c − wi . If the condition is satisﬁed, we drop item i from V since no feasible solutions containing
it can improve on the incumbent (of value LB). For each item i ∈ V,
we also consider the following second pegging condition:

LB ≥ UB(V \ {i}, c ),

(23)

where as upper bound we adopt the one which is the tightest on
the problem instance restricted to the items in Vࢨ{i} and to a capacity of c. If the condition is satisﬁed, we drop item i from V and
add it to the (partial) root-node solution Iˆ. This is correct since any
solution which can improve on the incumbent must contain it.
6.2. Implementation details
During the exploration of the branching tree, CFS examines the
vertex set Vˆ induced by the branching operations (see Section 4),
attempting to prune the corresponding node by relying on the upper bounds proposed in Section 5. More precisely, it ﬁrst computes
the bound UBL2 (Vˆ ) (18) (which can be read from the MCKP-based
lookup-table). Next, it computes the bound UBMT (Vˆ ) (15). If both
bounds are insuﬃcient to prune the node, the PARTITION procedure is called to compute a clique partition of the entire set of vertices Vˆ . Here, PARTITION is run by ignoring the condition on the
budget, thereby preventing the latter from halting the procedure
if the introduction of any extra items were to make the budget
value negative. Finally, we compute the bound UBP (Vˆ ) (21) based
on the obtained partition.
If all these attempts fail, we resort to the branching operations described in Section 4.1. Such operations are all based on the
branching and pruned sets B and P constructed by the PARTITION
procedure. The nodes of the branch-and-bound tree are explored in
a depth-ﬁrst fashion. The recursion naturally stops whenever the
branching set B is empty.
Finally, let us mention that our implementation relies on a very
eﬃcient bitstring representation to encode the conﬂict graph G as
well as the sets of vertices Vˆ and the clique partitions.

7. Computational experience
In this section, we present the results of an extensive computational campaign carried out to assess the performance of our new
branch-and-bound algorithm CFS. Our main goal is threefold:
(i) comparing CFS to the ILP formulations presented in
Section 3 (Section 7.1.1)
(ii) evaluating the strength and the impact of the different
bounding procedures used by CFS (Sections 7.1.2 and 7.1.3)
(iii) comparing the performance of CFS to the state-of-the-art
algorithm from the literature introduced in Bettinelli et al.
(2017) which, in the following, we refer to as BCM; the implementation of BCM has been kindly provided by the authors of Bettinelli et al. (2017) (Section 7.1.4).

The experiments are run on a 20-core Intel(R) Xeon(R) CPU
E5-2690 v2@3.00 gigahertz, with 128 gigabytes of main memory,

implemented in C++, compiled with gcc 4.8.4 (with the -o3
optimization setting), and run on a single core. On this machine,
the performance of the algorithm dfmax, commonly used for comparing the eﬃciency of machines with different hardware, is of
0.189, 1.155, and 4.369 seconds for the benchmark graphs r300.5,

CPU times are reported in seconds. On each instance and each run,
we adopt a time limit of 600 seconds. The ILP formulations are
solved using the state-of-the art commercial MILP solver CPLEX,
version 12.8.0 (called just CPLEX in what follows), run in singlethreaded mode, leaving all its parameters to their default value.
As main testbed for our experiments, we consider the instances
tested in Bettinelli et al. (2017), which feature conﬂict graphs with
nine values of density, ranging from 0.1 to 0.9. The same set of
instances has been considered in other works, including Elhedhli
et al. (2011), Muritiba et al. (2010), and Sadykov and Vanderbeck
(2013). In particular, all these instances were obtained starting
from the Bin Packing Problem instances proposed in Falkenauer
(1996). We refer to this testbed as the main testbed (results are
reported in Section 7.1).
Along the lines of Bettinelli et al. (2017), we also consider
a second testbed containing instances on which BCM, which is
a combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithm, is outperformed by
the branch-and-cut algorithm based on mathematical programming techniques implemented in CPLEX. Similarly to BCM, CFS is
not as eﬃcient as CPLEX on these instances (while still resulting
more eﬃcient than BCM). We report on the experiments on those
instances in Section 7.2. We refer to this testbed as the very sparse
testbed.
7.1. Results on the main testbed
The main testbed features instances belonging to one of eight
types and to one of two classes. Each of them is obtained adopting one of four capacity multipliers. The type speciﬁes the number
of items and their weight structure, the class speciﬁes the proﬁt
structure, and the capacity multiplier speciﬁes the value by which
the capacity is scaled. In all instances of type one to four, the items
have weights uniformly distributed in [20, 100] and the (unscaled)
knapsack capacity c is equal to 150. In them, the number of items
n (equal to the number of vertices of the conﬂict graph |V|) is 120,

250, 50 0, and 1,0 0 0, respectively. Instances of types ﬁve to eight
feature items with weights uniformly distributed in [250, 500] and
c equal to 1,0 0 0. For these instances, the number of items n is
60, 120, 349, and 501, and each triplet of items forms an exact
packing (i.e., it perfectly ﬁts the knapsack, saturating its capacity).
The two classes specify the way the item proﬁts are determined.
The instances of the ﬁrst class, the random class, which we identify by the letter “R”, feature items with random proﬁts uniformly
distributed in [1, 100]. The instances of the second class, the correlated class, which we identify by the letter “C”, feature items with
correlated proﬁts equal to pi = wi + 10, i = 1, . . . , n. As to the capacity multiplier, according to Bettinelli et al. (2017) we consider
instances with: (i) the original capacity (150 or 1,0 0 0, based on the
instance type) (ii) three times the original capacity, and (iii) ten
times the original capacity. Since high values of the knapsack capacity c tend to make the instances harder to solve, in this work
we also consider a fourth setting leading to more challenging instances: (iv) ﬁfteen times the original capacity. When referring to
an instance in the main testbed, the natural number that follows
the letter C or R identiﬁes the multiplier adopted for the knapsack
capacity. For each choice of type, class, and multiplier, the testbed
contains 90 instances (10 instances for each of the 9 density values
of the conﬂict graph), comprising, overall, 5760 instances.

7.1.2. Assessing the strength of the upper bounds adopted in CFS
We now assess the strength of the different bounds we introduced in Section 5, aiming at understanding which ones are better suited for their adoption in our algorithm CFS. For this experiment, we take into account a subset of instances of the main
testbed of both classes C and R, of types 1, 4, 5, and 8, and with
capacity multiplier of value 1 and 15.
Using the LP relaxation LP2 as baseline, we compare the tightness of two KP-based upper bounds, i.e., the Martello–Toth bound
UBMT (15) and the KP-based lookup-table bound UBL1 (14), and of
four MCKP-based upper bounds, i.e., the MCKP-based lookup-table
bound UBL2 (18), the partition-based bound UBP (21), the bound
obtained by solving to optimality the LP relaxation of the MCKP
relaxation of the KPC (18), which we refer to here as UBP˜ , and the
bound obtained by solving to optimality the unrestricted (i.e., with
integer variables) version of the MCKP relaxation of the KPC (16),
which we refer to here as UBP∗ . The latter two are computed via
the MCKNAP.c code developed by Pissinger, which is available online at the address hjemmesider.diku.dk/~pisinger/codes.html.
It is important to mention that, while U BMT , U BL1 , U BL2 , and
UBL2 can all be computed in a negligible amount of computing
time, U BP˜ and U BP∗ requiring a higher computational effort. Since
in CFS these bounds are employed as a pruning mechanism at
each node of the branch-and-bound tree, in order to obtain an efﬁcient algorithm it is crucial that the time spent to compute the
bounds is extremely small.
We measure the quality of the upper bound in Tables 5 and 6.
The results are reported aggregated by class, capacity multiplier,
and type in Table 5, and by class, capacity multiplier, and density
in Table 6. For each combination of these, we report the average
results obtained over nine instances (one per value of density) in
Table 5, and over eight instances (one per type) in Table 6.
As to the KP-based bounds, the tables show that UBMT is often
close to LP2 when the capacity multiplier is equal to 1, whereas
UBMT becomes substantially worse than LP2 when the capacity
multiplier is equal to 15 (on, in particular, instances of type 1, 4,
and 5). UBL1 is, by construction, at least as tight as UBMT and, as
the table shows, their difference, while observable for low-capacity
instances with a multiplier of 1 and a large density, is not too
pronounced. UBL1 turns out to be stronger than LP2 on instances
with a small capacity multiplier and, in particular, a low density.
As to the MCKP-based bounds, the tables show that these are always tighter than the KP-based ones (UBL2 is, by construction, at
least as tight as UBL1 ), and that they are much stronger than LP2 .

instances.

whereas BCM fails to solve 29 of them.
One of the main reasons behind the very good performance
of CPLEX with ILP1 is that the LP relaxation of ILP1 provides a
very strong upper bound when the conﬂict graph is very sparse.
Indeed, the LP-relaxation gap of ILP1 , ILP2 , and ILP3 is typically
much tighter on the sparse instances than the gap we measured
on the main testbed. On average, it is equal to, respectively, 2.8%,
10.8%, and 1.6%. The average computing times are 0.05 seconds
for ILP1 , negligible for ILP2 , and 0.18 seconds for ILP3 . We remark
that our algorithm heavily relies on the conﬂict graph for reducing the search space after each branching operation. When the
graph is extremely sparse, such reduction is often very modest.
Moreover, the bounds offered by the bounding procedures that

we use, i.e., U BMT , U BL2 , and UBP , are weaker than the three LPrelaxation bounds, being equal, on average, to 15.8%, 5.4%, 5.6%, respectively. It is worth noticing that, when the conﬂict graph is very
sparse, the clique partition adopted in the MCKP-based bounds is
very likely to contain very small cliques, thus only capturing a
fraction of the conﬂicts in the graph (only those among items in
the same cliques), resulting in a weak bound. For these reasons,
sparse graphs with densities between 0.01 and 0.05 are not suitable for being solved with our purely combinatorial branch-andbound algorithm CFS. On the contrary, when the conﬂict graph is
extremely sparse, with densities up to 0.005, the conﬂicts are almost irrelevant and the KP-based bounds are suﬃciently strong,
which allows CFS to achieve a performance comparable to that of
CPLEX with ILP1 .
7.3. Experiments with a hybrid algorithm
Table 10 shows that CPLEX with ILP1 is very effective on the
very sparse testbed. For this reason, we have developed a hybrid
algorithm, which we refer to as CFS+LP, that combines CFS with
the LP-based upper bound that is computed by CPLEX within its
branch-and-cut tree. In this algorithm, we employ the LP relaxation
of ILP1 (solved on the graph induced by the vertex set Vˆ ) to prune
the branch-and-bound nodes that are generated within CFS. This
additional pruning procedure is called only if pruning fails with
each of the upper bounds adopted in CFS.
On the very sparse random instances, we observe that CFS+LP
outperforms CFS, managing to solve 209 instances as opposed
to 176. Differently, on the very sparse correlated instances CFS
achieves a better performance, as CFS+LP only solves 145 instances, whereas CFS solves 156. Our experiments reveal that, on
the very sparse random instances, the LP-based bound is successful in pruning a node in 74% of the cases in which it is called.
Differently, on the very sparse correlated instances, this happens
in only 5.9% of the cases. This shows that, on the latter instances,
computing this LP-based upper bound almost always results in
an overhead. Empirically, the pruning capability of such an upper bound is indeed very limited. This results in an increase in
the computing time for processing each branch-and-bound node,
which, ultimately, leads to solving fewer instances to optimality
within the time limit. We remark that the bound provided by
the LP relaxation is not the only strength of CPLEX, as many
other of its features, such as preprocessing, cutting plane generation, and primal heuristics are all necessary for the effectiveness
of CPLEX’s branch-and-cut algorithm for this set of very sparse instances.
In order to assess if, thanks to the hybridization, CFS+LP can
lead to an improvement beyond the very sparse testbed, we also
test it on the denser subset of 240 instances taken from the main
testbed considered in Section 7.1.3 and Table 8, with densities
ranging from 0.1 to 0.5. On these instances, our tests show that
CFS remains the better-performing algorithm, as it outperforms
CFS+LP. Indeed, CFS manages to solve 225 out of 240 instances,
as opposed to CFS+LP, which manages to solve to proven optimality only 209 of them. This result is in accordance with what
we observed on the correlated instances in the very sparse testbed.
In particular, it shows that the branch-and-bound algorithm, CFS,
proposed in this paper—which, due to being purely combinatorial,
does not rely on bounds based on mathematical programming—is
the best choice for instances with densities greater than or equal
to 0.1.
8. Conclusions
In this paper, we have addressed the Knapsack Problem with
Conﬂicts (KPC), which is an important generalization of the clas-

S. Coniglio, F. Furini and P. San Segundo / European Journal of Operational Research 289 (2021) 435–455

sical Knapsack Problem to the case with conﬂicts between pairs
of items. The KPC is particularly relevant as it often appears as
a subproblem when solving other well-known combinatorial optimization problems with methods based on column generation,
such as, e.g., the Bin Packing Problem or the Bin Packing Problem
with Conﬂict Graph. The KPC is computationally very challenging,
and solving it via methods based on integer programming is hard
for graphs with densities between 0.1 and 0.9. In this work, we
have proposed a new purely-combinatorial branch-and-bound algorithm, called CFS, which relies on the notions of branching and
pruned sets and on a collection of bounding procedures, based on
different relaxations, for fathoming the nodes of the implicit enumeration tree. This includes, in particular, bounds based on the
Multiple-Choice Knapsack Problem and the dual of its linear programming relaxation, for which we have introduced a fast heuristic procedure. We have extensively assessed the performance of
CFS on a large testbed of instances typically adopted in the literature. The results show that, for graphs with densities equal to
0.1 or more, our algorithm clearly outperforms the state-of-the-art
method proposed in Bettinelli et al. (2017) as well as three differ-

ent integer programming formulations (ILP1 , ILP2 , and ILP3 ) solved
with CPLEX. The speed up is, in some cases, by up to several orders of magnitude in terms of computing times. On the other hand,
for graphs with a density strictly smaller than 0.1, we suggest the
adoption of a state-of-the-art ILP solver such as CPLEX, employing
the formulation ILP1 .
Future lines of research may include the extension of the ideas
proposed in this paper to other generalizations of the Knapsack
Problem with an underlying graph, modeling, for example, precedence constraints, such as the Knapsack Problem with Precedences,
see, e.g., Boland, Bley, Fricke, Froyland, and Sotirov (2012).
